Engineering Undergrad

Dev Bhatia

Class of 2021

My reflection on the undergrad engineering experience. I came across a few of these articles written by others when I was starting college and found them helpful (so here I am, doing the same to share my perspective with others in a position I was in before). A little bit about me - I studied electrical engineering at UC San Diego, was part of some student orgs, worked on-campus, volunteered at a neuroscience research lab, and held internships at Intel, Tesla, and Microsoft during my undergrad. Obviously, this is all my opinion.
"Be T Shaped" was one of the best pieces of advice I got near the end of my freshman year. The horizontal bar at the top of the "T" represents all that you know, and the vertical bar in the middle represents the depth to which you know something. I've observed that in most engineering curriculums at UCSD, students gain an in-depth understanding of their major only. This is not sufficient.Â 

Generally there are three main fields of engineering (software, electronics, and mechanical), within which there are different majors/disciplines. I know there's also bio/chem/nano and many others that do not fit into these, but the point is don't limit yourself to just one. For example, if you major within software engineering, complement that with some electronics fundamentals ("People who really care about software, build their own hardware" or something like that).

I could pair different majors and go into how they complement one another, but what I've observed is to have a good understanding of the digital (software, computing) and physical (electrical hardware, robotics, structures) worlds as much as you can.

These are some books I found helpful during that time. Some are technical, others career oriented, and some just for fun.

My Inventions by Nikola Tesla
In the fast-paced and dynamic world of tech, sometimes we forgot what it means to invent or why even continue studying engineering - this short autobiography reinstills the spirit of innovation.

The No BS Guide to Linear Algebra by Ivan Savov
A very striaghforward approach to learning one of the most fundemental topics in math. At the very least, you will learn how to "read" math. Knowing how to read math will expedite your ability to grasp concepts, definitions, and theorems in your couses.

Cracking the PM Interview by Gayle Laakmann McDowell & Jackie Bavaro
Regardless of your intent to purse the PM role or your ability to engineer solutions, great teams indirectly seek out people who can access the impact and reach of their work both internally for the team and externally for users. This book helps you develop that mindest through real examples and clear guidance.

It's Not How Good You Are, It's How Good You Want To Be by Paul Arden
Short, simple, and well worth it. Takes about 20-30 minutes to read. The title says it all.

48 Laws of Power by Robert Greene
Just the laws and descriptions, not the whole book.
