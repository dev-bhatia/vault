Engineering Undergrad

Dev Bhatia

Class of 2021

This is my reflection on the undergrad engineering experience and some stuff I learned along the way. A little bit about me - I study electrical engineering at UC San Diego, was part of some student orgs, worked on-campus at the Career Center, volunteered at a neuroscience research lab, and held internships at Tesla, Microsoft, & Intel during my undergrad. Obviously, this is all just my opinion.
Currently being updated every few days.


"Be T Shaped" was one of the best pieces of advice I got near the end of my freshman year. The horizontal bar at the top of the "T" represents all that you know, and the vertical bar in the middle represents the depth to which you know something. I've observed that in most engineering curriculums at UCSD, students gain an in-depth understanding of their major only. This is not sufficient.

Generally there are three main fields of engineering (software, electronics, and mechanical), within which there are different majors/disciplines. I know there's also bio/chem/nano and many others that do not fit into these, but the point is don't limit yourself to just one. For example, if you major within software engineering, complement that with some electronics fundamentals ("People who are really serious about software, should make their own hardware" - Alan Kay ).

Sure, I could pair different majors and go into how they complement one another, but ultimatley what it comes down to is to have a good understanding of the digital (software, computing) and physical (electrical hardware, robotics, structures) worlds as much as you can. I cannot stress this enough.

What is Federal Work Study This is one of those things that is super simple, but requires someone to explain it to you once (at least for me). A work study award is a specifed amount the government can pay you if you get an on campus job that supports work study. At this job, when you get your paycheck, the funds come from the government and not the university. That is until your award runs out. Then, your pay comes from the univeristy funds. For example, say you recieved a work study award for $1000 for the whole school year. You then got an on-campus job that pays $10 / hr. Your first quarter, you worked 100 hrs. So, you got paid $1000 in that quarter. That $1000 was paid by the government, fulfilling the work study award you have. However, the next quarter, you work another 100 hrs (also earning another $1000). But, since your work study award was fullfiled already the previous quarter, the pay for this quarter came from the univeristy department you work for. For you, this does not mean much since you still get paid for the hours your've worked. It is only a matter of where the funds come from to pay you. To learn more about work study, refer to this post from the US Dept. of Education.

Why? Reasons to work on campus differ for everyone. The 1.5 years I worked at the Career Center (3rd - 4th year) was super rewarding, apart from getting some extra money to pay for groceries, food, & commuting.

4 Year Plan(s) This is like any other plan...it will change. Two quarters into my first year, I stopped following the recommended four year plan and made my own instead. The reason: I wanted more project based classes with hands-on learning rather than learning only theory. There are pros and cons to deviating from the recommended plan. The con: you don't take classes consistently with the same people your year (the consequence being you have to make more of an effort to get to know people each quarter in your classes). The pro: you can prioritize classes based on your needs (project based classes, graduating early, etc). Looking back, taking project based or application focused classes early helps build up your resume with tangible skills and you become more capable of builings things sooner, rather than later. Do not blindly follow recommended plans.

(most) textbooks are a scam Always avoid purchasing textbooks. Two reasons: professors barely use them expect for assigning homework & you'll end up learning more from YouTube videos anyways. You can most likely (~95% chance) find a PDF of the book online. I want to be very clear - I am not encouraging this, just stating a probablity :)

Resume Advice After 1.5 years reviewing resumes at the UCSD Career Center, these is what I'd recommend to anybody creating a Resume or CV.
  • 1 Page in Length. 1-2 Pages for Masters Students (thoguht try to fit it in 1). And for PhD's/PostDocs's you are now creating a CV, so any reasonable length is fine.
  • PDF format, but make it on Microsoft Word. ATS systems seem to be able to parse PDFs converted form Word better than those from Pages or Google Docs.
  • Avoid color. If you really need it try grayscale first. Some companies print resumes, and your resume may look drasticlaly different when printed in black & white versus in color.
  • Bulleted descriptions should only be only line in length. If you really need a second line, it should fill up at least 1/2 of the second line.
  • Use tables to format sections on your resumes. This will reduce white-space and give you more flexibility to organize your content. After putting all the text, and formatting the spacing in each cell, set the line width to 0 to hide the cell outline.
  • Single column layouts work better than two column ones. This is from trial & error.
  • Font sizes for descriptions should be between 10 - 12 pt.

UCSD College System UCSD has 7 colleges at the time of this writing. No matter what anybody tells you - no, they are not like the houses in Hogwarts. Colleges only matter for two things - where you live on-campus (if you decide to) and what your GEs will be. The latter is more important as most of campus is pretty nice. Sixth has the easiest GEs, Muir and Warren are tied for second, Marshall is just there (very neutral), ERC and Seventh have too many required writing classes.

Writing GEs Regardless of what college you end up in, everyone has to take a writing sequence as part of their GEs. Use them to boost your GPA as an engineering major. It all comes down to your TAs. These people have the most influence on your grades. In writing classes, go to their office hours occasionally to get feedback on your work (when, not if, you make their suggested changes) you will more likely get a higher score than if you didn't go.

My Inventions by Nikola Tesla
Sometimes we forgot what it means to invent or why even continue studying engineering - this short autobiography reinstills the spirit of innovation & excitement to engineer.

The No B.S. Guide to Linear Algebra by Ivan Savov
A very striaghforward approach to learning one of the most fundemental topics in math. At the very least, you will learn how to "read" math. Knowing how to read math will expedite your ability to grasp concepts, definitions, and theorems in coursework.

Cracking the PM Interview by Gayle Laakmann McDowell & Jackie Bavaro
Regardless of your intent to purse the PM role or your ability to engineer solutions, great teams indirectly seek out people who can access the impact and reach of their work both internally for the team and externally for users. This book helps you develop that mindest through real examples and clear guidance.

It's Not How Good You Are, It's How Good You Want To Be by Paul Arden
Short and humorous. Takes ~20 to 30 minutes to read. Worth every page.

48 Laws of Power by Robert Greene
Just the laws and their descriptions in the Table of Contents.
